How I Came to this Fork in the Road

The path to becoming a full stack developer was an unexpected journey.

Some background on how my love for game-based learning led me here:
Beginning a new teaching position where curriculum resources weren't easily accessible set me searching for ways to address the following: how do you help 6th graders deeply understand what visual-spatial intelligence is while they are beginnning to develop abstract reasoning skills? I drew on experience from being exposed to challenging games from working in a specialty toy store years earlier. I believe in the adage "Learn by doing". I believe that experience creates deep learning opportunities. I introduced the game of Set to my middle school learners as a way to show them one experience of drawing on visual-spatial strengths.
I was afforded the opportunity to attend a National Association for Gifted Children conference in Indianapolis, & started reading Mary Cay Ricci's Mindsets in the Classroom on the plane ride home. When I came across a section which talked about how brain-based learning infused with critical thinking game play helped nurture a growth mindset, I felt validated about incorporating games in the classroom. One of my bosses, D51 Gifted-talented Director, Heather Baskin, supported my ideas about creating a competitive space which included the use of games in the classroom. My middle school gifted-talented colleagues also liked the idea. The result included approval of games purchased for eight middle schools, and a collaborative effort to bring a new "sport" to life for our gifted-talented youth. We organized a tournament which I facilitated at the middle school where I taught (Bookcliff Middle School). After our first annual tournament was a great success, Ms. Baskin authorized the purchase of a tournament game set, which I gladly organized & executed. We aspired to holding the Scholastica Tournament two times a year. There's entirely too many syllables in the word "Scholastica", but that's the name I gave it!
Double-dog dare you to claim that you wouldn't make the same face while lying on a bed of nails! My boss took this picture at the closing reception for the 61st Annual Convention of the National Association for Gifted Children in Baltimore, MD. I learned so much at the first national conference I attended, that I took Ms. Baskin's dare regarding future attendance: those who presented were guaranteed slots that the district sponsored. I was doing online master's studies in gifted education, and had just hosted the first Scholastica tournament. My first national conference presentation was about how to incorporate Scholastica tournaments in middle school gifted programming services. And, at the end of the conference... I laid down on a bed of nails at the Maryland Science Center!

A local news station covered a tournament with the title Scholastica Creates New Ways to Learn. This may have been our 2nd district wide Scholastica Tournament out of the 4 or 5 I facilitated. D51 is still hosting the event. After every tournament I looked forward to incorporating feedback from competitors, parents, colleagues who helped make the event a success, & youth who were starting to sign up for a leadership opportunity to help run it. I took notes on all the feedback and was able to introduce new aspects to the event each time round. For example, the game competition bracket seemed to always need tweaks after each tournament. Also, we adjusted the tournament schedule to include time for non-competitive game play so that youth would have the opportunity to get to know peers from other schools in the community in a non-competitive setting.
This video covers the last Scholastica Tournament I facilitated. I hosted two tournaments that year. Some great improvements included: 1) building the youth leadership team which would help facilitate the event & prepare by hosting a training event prior to each tournament, 2) youth leading "Options" game time after competitive gameplay for interactive fun, 3) having a theme for adult & youth leadership which united us in a fun way & also gave a visual cue to competitors about who to ask for fun, 4) incorporating new games each time, 5) tweaking the game bracket each time, 6) building an Excel spreadsheet (w/the help of my husband) which would execute the calculations for individual & school winners, 7) inviting participation from the local toy store owner to be involved in a variety of ways (& she was involved w/such a dedicated, generous, & servant-minded spirit!!), 8) inviting students from my school to help w/the clean up after the tournament w/a challenge to finish w/enough time to have an after tournament game together.. which led to the Epic Rubber Band War (rules created & voted on by participants)... and I'm sure there's more which created a sense of tradition & tribe surrounding the event.. but those are what stick out in my mind!
My story...

It is amazing.. what can happen in a handful of years with a team of dedicated people who want to be innovative about making life and learning experiences meaningful!

After presenting at several conferences about Scholastica Tournaments, people were reaching out to me to learn more about how to host tournaments in their own schools. I thought it would be best to create a website to direct people to as a more efficient way to collaborate as compared to email correspondence. I took a class one of the local colleges to learn how to build Wordpress sites better. My teacher referenced the purpose of my site as a small business, and that got me thinking...

Since then I decided to invest in a 14 week General Assembly web development immersive bootcamp to learn more about how to build sites using a variety of coding languages and technologies. I fell in love with the process of building tools that address life processes with efficiency. I've been continuing to build my skills since then!
